*There's a cost to AI delegation that nobody puts in the product brochure. It isn't
about accuracy. It isn't about privacy. It's about something more quietly fundamental:
when you stop doing tasks, you stop remembering how to do them. And memory, it turns
out, isn't just something you have — it's something you build, through the act of
doing itself.*

## The Notebook You Actually Remember

Think back to school. A friend shares their notes before an exam. Neatly organized,
everything you need. You read through them carefully. You understand everything on the
page. Then you close the notebook, and by tomorrow, most of it has evaporated.

Now think about the time you sat down and wrote your own notes — slowly, in your own
words, reconstructing the lecture from memory and filling gaps as you went. It was
slower. It was harder. But a week later, you still knew what you wrote.

This isn't a coincidence or a quirk of individual learning style. It's a well-documented
cognitive phenomenon: **the act of generating information yourself encodes it far more
durably than the act of receiving it.** Psychologists call this the *Generation Effect*.
Studied extensively since the late 1970s, it consistently shows that information you
produce — even through something as simple as completing a word, writing a sentence, or
solving a step — is remembered significantly better than information you passively read
or hear.

The implication for AI is uncomfortable: when AI generates the output and you review it,
you are in the reader's position. When you produce it yourself, even imperfectly, you
are in the writer's position. These are not equivalent. One of them builds memory. The
other mostly doesn't.

## We've Seen This Before — But AI Goes Further

The concern about outsourcing memory to technology isn't new. When search engines became
ubiquitous, researchers identified what they called the *Google Effect*: people invest
less effort in remembering information they believe they can easily retrieve from a
device. Why encode something in your own memory when it's one search away?

The effect is real and measurable. But it's also arguably acceptable — reference
information (what year was the Treaty of Westphalia signed, what's the syntax for a
regex lookahead) is genuinely better stored in searchable systems than in biological
memory. We've always used external tools to offload information we don't need to carry
in our heads.

AI changes this equation in a crucial way: **it doesn't just store information for you,
it performs tasks for you.** The difference is significant.

Knowing that you can look up the capital of a country is not the same as knowing that
AI can write your code, structure your arguments, plan your project, and draft your
communications. The first offloads facts. The second offloads *processes* — the very
cognitive activities through which expertise, judgment, and deep competence are built.

---

## Expertise Isn't Stored, It's Accumulated

Here's what makes this particularly insidious: expertise in any domain isn't really a
collection of facts you've memorized. It's a web of *experiences of doing* — problems
encountered, mistakes made, solutions found, patterns recognized over time.

A senior engineer doesn't know how to architect a system because they read about
architecture. They know because they've built systems, watched them fail, debugged them
at 2am, and internalized the gap between theory and reality through repeated, effortful
engagement. A skilled writer doesn't produce good prose because they've read good prose.
They produce it because they've written badly, revised, written again, and slowly
developed an internal sense of what works.

This accumulated experience doesn't transfer from a summary. It doesn't transfer from
watching an AI do the task. It transfers through *doing*.

If AI begins handling the hard, effortful parts of a domain — the debugging, the
drafting, the structuring, the analysis — at the early stages of someone's career or
learning journey, they may produce acceptable outputs in the short term while
systematically failing to build the experiential foundation that produces genuine
expertise over the long term. They may become fluent in directing AI without ever
becoming fluent in the underlying domain.

## The Expanding Footprint Problem

Right now, AI is useful in specific, bounded contexts. You might use it for writing
assistance, for code suggestions, for summarizing documents. The cognitive cost is
real but contained.

The more interesting — and more concerning — question is about trajectory. AI is
expanding its footprint rapidly, and the vision many AI companies are building toward
is one where AI agents handle not just individual tasks but extended workflows, ongoing
projects, and eventually large portions of both professional and personal life.

The more tasks you delegate, the smaller the domain of experience you actually
accumulate. And this compounds. Each area you stop engaging with directly is an area
where your memory, intuition, and judgment fail to develop or gradually atrophy. You
become, over time, a coordinator of AI outputs rather than a practitioner of anything.

There's a word for what remains when you have managed many things but mastered none of
them: **shallowness**. Not in a moral sense, but in a structural one. Wide awareness,
thin depth. You know enough to prompt, not enough to truly evaluate.

---

## This Isn't an Argument Against AI

Let me be clear about what I'm not arguing. I'm not saying AI is bad, or that
delegation is inherently harmful, or that we should deliberately choose difficulty for
its own sake. Tools that reduce drudgery, eliminate repetition, and amplify human
capability are genuinely valuable.

The argument is more specific: **the cognitive tax of AI delegation is real, unequally
distributed across task types, and almost entirely invisible in current discourse.** It
isn't reflected in how AI tools are designed, marketed, or adopted. Nobody is measuring
what you stop remembering. Nobody is tracking the expertise you don't accumulate.

And as AI becomes more capable and more embedded in daily life, the question of what
humans are still *doing* — and therefore still *building* — becomes increasingly
important. Not because AI doing things is bad, but because humans not doing things has
consequences that compound quietly over years, not loudly over days.

---

## A Different Way to Think About AI Integration

If the Memory Tax is real, then the most responsible approach to AI adoption isn't
"automate everything you can" — it's something more like: **be intentional about what
you keep doing yourself.**

Some questions worth asking when you reach for an AI tool:

- Is this a task where doing it myself would build something in me, or is it purely
  mechanical repetition?
- Am I using AI here because it genuinely does this better, or because doing it myself
  feels uncomfortable and slow?
- If AI does this for me consistently, will I still be able to do it myself in a year?
  Does that matter?
- Is this a domain where I want to develop deep competence, or is it genuinely a
  peripheral task?

None of these questions have universal answers. Delegating formatting and boilerplate
is not the same as delegating reasoning and judgment. The key is developing the
awareness to tell the difference — and making active choices rather than defaulting to
automation whenever it's available.

---

## Closing Thought

There's a version of the AI future that looks extraordinarily productive from the
outside: everything runs smoothly, outputs are polished, tasks are completed. And
underneath it, a generation of people who are increasingly unsure what they actually
know how to do — because for a long time now, they haven't had to do very much.

That outcome isn't inevitable. But it's also not being designed against, at least not
intentionally. The responsibility right now falls largely on individuals: to treat AI
as a tool that should serve their growth, not substitute for it. To keep doing the
things that build them, even when AI could do those things faster.

The notebook you wrote yourself is still the one you remember.
