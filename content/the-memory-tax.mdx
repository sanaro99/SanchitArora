There is a cost to letting AI do the work, and it’s not one you’ll find in the product
brochure. It isn't about privacy or accuracy. It’s about memory.

When you stop doing tasks, you stop remembering how to do them. And memory, it turns
out, isn't just something you *have*—it's something you *build* through the act of doing.

---

## The Notebook Effect

Think back to your student days. Remember that feeling when a friend shared their notes
with you? They were perfect, organized, and legible. You read them. You understood them.
And then, by the next day, the information was gone.

Now compare that to the time you wrote your own notes. You had to struggle a bit to
phrase things. You scribbled in the margins. It was slower. But a week later, you
still knew it.

This is the **Generation Effect**. Psychologists have known about it since the 70s:
information you generate yourself is stuck in your brain far more securely than
information you passively consume. When you write the code, draft the essay, or solve
the problem, you are building neural pathways.

When AI does it and you just review it? You’re just a tourist.

---

## The Google Effect on Steroids

We saw a version of this with search engines. The "Google Effect" showed that people
stop remembering facts when they know they can just look them up. And honestly? That
was fine. You don't need to memorize the date of the Treaty of Westphalia.

But AI goes further. It doesn’t just store facts; it **performs processes**.

Knowing you can look up a capital city is one thing. Knowing that AI can structure your
argument, plan your project, or debug your code is different. The first offloads data.
The second offloads the *struggle*—the very thing that builds expertise.

Expertise isn't a database of facts. It’s a web of experiences. A senior engineer
knows how to fix a system because they’ve broken it a dozen times. A great writer knows
what works because they’ve written terrible drafts and fixed them.

If AI handles the drafting, the debugging, and the structuring, you get the result, but
you skip the workout. You might produce great work in the short term, but you stop
building the muscle that makes you an expert.

---

## The Shallowing

Right now, this is manageable. AI helps us with small tasks. But as agents get better,
they will take over longer workflows.

The more you delegate, the less deep experience you accumulate. This creates a risk of
**shallowing**. You become a coordinator—someone who knows enough to prompt the AI, but
not enough to do the work yourself. You have wide awareness, but thin depth.

I’m not saying we should ban AI or make work hard for no reason. Drudgery is drudgery;
let the machines have it. But we need to be honest about the trade-off.

---

## Be Intentional About What You Do

So, how do we live with this?

We have to be intentional. We can't just default to "automate everything." We have to
ask: **Is this a task that builds me?**

*   If I automate this, will I lose a skill I care about?
*   Am I handing this off because it’s a waste of time, or just because it’s hard?
*   Do I want to be an expert in this, or just a manager of it?

There is no right answer for everyone. Delegating email responses? Go for it.
Delegating the core thinking of your profession? Be careful.

The future of work might look incredibly productive on the outside. But if we aren't
careful, underneath that surface efficiency, we might forget how to do the very things
that make us valuable.

The notebook you write yourself is still the one you remember.
