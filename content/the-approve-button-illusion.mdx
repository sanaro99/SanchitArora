Everyone in the AI industry seems to agree on one thing: we need "humans in the loop."
Keep people involved, the thinking goes, and you keep the system safe, accountable, and
trustworthy.

It sounds responsible. But look at how it’s actually implemented in most products, and
you’ll see a problem. The "loop" isn't a loop. It’s just a button.

---

## A Button Is Not a Decision

Imagine two scenarios.

**Scenario A:** An AI agent writes a complex piece of code for your app. It changes how
user authentication works, modifies database schemas, and updates security protocols.
It shows you a summary: "Auth logic updated. Ready to merge." There is one button: **Approve**.

**Scenario B:** An AI assistant drafts your weekly grocery list based on what you usually
buy. Eggs, milk, bread. It asks: "Ready to order?" There is one button: **Approve**.

The interface is identical. The physical action—clicking a button—is identical. But
the stakes? Completely different. One approval could introduce a massive security
vulnerability. The other might get you the wrong brand of oat milk.

This is the **Approve Button Illusion**. We are flattening every type of human oversight
into a single, generic interaction that *feels* like control but provides almost none
of it.

---

## The "Summary" Trap

The standard defense is, "Well, the human reviewed the summary before clicking."

Did they, though?

Reading a three-sentence summary of a code migration is not the same as understanding
it. To genuinely own that decision, you have to read the diff, understand the logic,
and mentally simulate what happens if it fails. A summary screen discourages that deep
work. It invites you to skim and click.

Worse, it creates false confidence. When something breaks later, the instinct is,
"But I approved it!" The button becomes a shield—a way to feel accountable without
actually doing the work of accountability.

---

## Control Must Match the Task

If we want humans to actually be in the loop, the control mechanism has to match the
stakes of the task.

For groceries? Sure, a button is fine. The cost of failure is low.

For deploying code or medical advice? A button is not enough. Genuine ownership requires
friction. The interface should demand that you engage. Maybe you have to answer a
question about the downstream effects before you can proceed. Maybe you have to explicitly
sign off on specific risk factors.

The AI can do the heavy lifting, but the human needs to engage with the result as
deeply as the consequences demand. One button cannot carry that weight for every
situation.

---

## Why We Design It This Way

Why is the "Approve" button so ubiquitous? Because it’s cheap and it makes us feel better.

Companies want a record that a human signed off (for liability). Users want to feel like
they aren't just passive passengers. A button satisfies both needs with zero friction.
But it’s a design shortcut. It gives us the appearance of oversight while quietly eroding
the reality of it.

---

## Fixing the Loop

We need to stop treating "approval" as a binary switch. Good AI design should differentiate:

1.  **Low stakes?** Simple confirm.
2.  **High stakes?** Forced reasoning. Ask the user, "What happens if this variable is null?" If they can't answer, they haven't reviewed it.
3.  **Irreversible actions?** Make the UI *feel* heavy. Approving a database wipe shouldn't feel like ordering a pizza.

A single button does not make a human responsible for a decision. It just makes them a
witness to one. Those are very different things. If we keep confusing them, we’re going
to end up with systems where everyone technically approved everything, but nobody
actually understood anything.
