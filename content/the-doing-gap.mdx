There’s something strange happening with AI right now. We have systems that can write
code, analyze medical scans, and draft legal briefs—often faster than any human.
Technically, they are capable. Yet for the tasks that actually matter, we hesitate to
hand over the wheel.

The usual explanation is that we don't trust the technology yet. We’re worried about
hallucinations or bias. And sure, those are real problems. But I think that’s a
surface-level diagnosis. The real friction runs much deeper—all the way down to our
biological wiring.

The problem isn’t just that we don’t trust AI. It’s that **we need to feel like we did
the thing ourselves.**

Not just see it get done. Not just approve the result. We need to have been the agent
of its completion. This isn't irrationality or technophobia. It is, I’d argue, one of
the most fundamental instincts our species possesses—and AI is the first technology in
history to collide head-on with it.

---

## From Punch Cards to Prompts

Look at how we’ve interacted with computers over the last 70 years.

In the early days, "doing" meant toggling physical switches and loading punch cards.
It was brutal work. Then came programming languages, which made it easier but still
required you to write the logic. Then GUIs arrived—drag, click, save—and suddenly
millions of people could use computers without writing code.

At every step, the interface got easier. But the human was still *doing something*.
You typed the words. You dragged the file. You clicked the shutter. The loop was
intact: you formed an intention, you took an action, and you saw the result.

AI breaks this chain. You describe what you want, and something else executes it.
The result appears, and however good it is, a part of your brain doesn't fully own
it. It doesn’t register as *done* in the deep, satisfying way that a finished task
usually does.

That is the **Doing Gap**.

---

## Why "Doing" Matters

Why does this feeling matter? Because the act of doing delivers something that the
output alone never can.

**First, it gives you agency.** Neuroscience tells us that the "sense of agency"—the
feeling that you control your actions and their consequences—is how we build confidence.
When a task finishes because *you* pushed it across the line, your brain logs it as
proof of competence. When an agent does it, that log entry is blank.

**Second, we value what we build.** This is the "IKEA Effect." We irrationally value
furniture we assembled ourselves more than pre-assembled stuff, even if our craftsmanship
is slightly wobbly. The act of building generates ownership. But here’s the catch:
research shows this effect vanishes if you don't finish the task. It’s not about
effort; it’s about *performed completion*. AI outputs, no matter how polished, carry
zero psychological equity.

---

## Is This Hardwired?

Here is where it gets interesting. Why do we have this need? Why isn't the result enough?

It’s likely an evolutionary heuristic. For most of human history, you couldn't verify
that an outcome was safe unless you were the one who caused it. An organism that acted,
saw the result, and learned from it survived better than one that passively accepted
outcomes from the environment.

The drive to "act and verify" isn't a personality quirk. It’s a survival mechanism.
We are descended from creatures who needed to do things themselves to know the world
was safe.

---

## The Black Box Problem

You might say, "But we delegate all the time! We hire accountants and contractors."

True. But when you hire a contractor, you can watch them work. When you use a calculator,
you press the buttons. There is a *legible process*.

AI, specifically large language models, is a black box. You cannot watch it think. You
put in a prompt, and an answer materializes. Research suggests that this opacity is a
massive psychological barrier. We don't resist AI because the answers are wrong; we
resist it because we can't see the path it took to get there.

For the first time, we have a tool capable of replacing human effort but architecturally
incapable of showing us the work.

---

## So, What Do We Do?

If this resistance is biological, then the solution isn't just "make AI more accurate."
Accuracy is necessary, but it’s not sufficient.

We need a design philosophy built around **meaningful control**. We need interfaces
that let users experience themselves as the cause of the outcome. That means:

*   **Show the work:** Explainability features that reveal the reasoning, not just the answer.
*   **Real checkpoints:** Moments where the human has to make a genuine decision, not just rubber-stamp a result.
*   **Collaboration, not handoff:** Building *with* the AI, rather than just receiving a finished artifact.

The goal isn't to make AI slower. It's to redesign the interaction so that when the
task is done, your brain’s ancient accounting system gives you the credit.

Trust isn't just about trusting the machine. It's about trusting yourself—trusting that
you know what happened and that you’re actually in control. That need isn't a bug. It’s
what makes us human.
